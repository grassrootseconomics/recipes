# Recipes Economics

Recipes can be read as a small closed economy. Each cook begins with a stock of a real ingredient and a fixed number of promise cards for that ingredient. Promise cards circulate as claims on real ingredients. The Common Basket acts as a public clearing pool. Finished dishes become new food-piece assets that can also circulate during settlement.

The game creates an economy with production, exchange, credit, settlement, and consumption:

- Production: redeeming promise cards consumes real ingredient stock and fills recipe slots.
- Exchange: players trade cards and food pieces through the Common Basket or through direct offers.
- Credit: holding another player's promise card means holding a claim against that player's ingredient stock.
- Settlement: the table returns to the required final shape, with each cook holding their own remaining cards and having the required cards in the Common Basket.
- Consumption: food pieces are eaten only after settlement is complete.

## Economic Shape

The economy begins with symmetric endowments. Every cook owns one ingredient, starts with equal real stock, and contributes opening promise cards to the Common Basket. The recipes then create asymmetric demand because each cook needs a different basket of ingredients. A cook's own ingredient is useful, but not sufficient. Progress depends on moving claims to the cooks who need them.

The Common Basket is the main liquidity mechanism. It lowers search costs because a cook can swap against a public pool instead of always needing a matching direct counterparty. Direct offers still matter when the basket is thin or when a cook is holding a scarce card that another cook needs.

Finished dish pieces add a second settlement medium. They make it possible to return promise cards to their owners without requiring a perfect card-for-card match. In economic terms, food pieces become a produced settlement asset.

## Supply And Demand

Supply is constrained by real ingredient stock and by the number of promise cards. A promise card without remaining stock should not function as a credible claim. Demand is generated by recipes: every recipe requires six redeemed units, and every cook must finish three dishes. This makes total ingredient demand predictable from the catalog, while the timing and local scarcity are driven by player choices and bot decisions.

Scarcity appears when a requested ingredient is unavailable from the relevant holder. Scarcity does not always mean total supply is exhausted. It can also mean the asset is in the wrong place, locked in another offer, sitting in the Common Basket, or held by a player who has not yet been induced to trade.

## Settlement Capacity

Settlement capacity is the table's ability to return to the final accounting condition after production has moved cards through the economy. A healthy table has enough food pieces and basket liquidity for players to recover their own cards and clear foreign cards from their hands.

The important settlement question is not only "did every cook finish dishes?" It is also "can every outstanding claim be cleared?" If players have many foreign cards in hand and few useful food pieces in circulation, settlement may take many turns even after production is complete.

## Velocity

Velocity measures how actively assets move through the economy. High Common Basket velocity means the public pool is doing useful clearing work. High direct exchange share means bilateral trade is doing more of the coordination. Neither is automatically better. A fast, liquid basket can reduce negotiation friction, while direct exchange can solve specific shortages that a public pool cannot.

## Distribution

The bite totals at the end measure consumption distribution. If all food is eaten but bite counts vary widely, the table produced enough output but distributed it unevenly. This is why the game reports consumption variance. The game can therefore be evaluated as both a production economy and a distribution economy.

## Game Stats

The Game Stats panel reports two groups of metrics.

Core flow metrics:

- Player turns: the number of completed player turns.
- Cycles: player turns divided by active cooks. One cycle is one full round of turns.
- Interactions: all non-pass transactions.
- Opening offerings: automatic starting deposits into the Common Basket.
- Common Basket swaps: card or food-piece swaps through the public pool.
- Direct exchanges: accepted direct offers between cooks.
- Redemptions: promise cards redeemed into recipe ingredients.
- Dishes prepared: total dishes completed.
- Settlement swaps: swaps used during settlement.
- Food-piece settlement swaps: settlement swaps involving produced food pieces.
- Bites eaten: total consumed food pieces.
- Assets lost: real ingredient units consumed.
- Productivity: total eaten food pieces.
- Profit: productivity minus assets lost.
- Gain: profit divided by assets lost.

Economics metrics:

- Average turns per dish: player turns divided by dishes prepared. Lower values mean production moved faster.
- Average interactions per dish: non-pass interactions divided by dishes prepared. This measures coordination cost per unit of output.
- Basket velocity: Common Basket swaps plus settlement swaps per cycle. This measures public-pool throughput.
- Direct exchange share: direct exchanges divided by total trades. This shows how much trade is bilateral rather than basket-mediated.
- Settlement burden: settlement swaps divided by total interactions. This estimates how much of the economy is spent clearing debts.
- Scarcity pressure per ingredient: unavailable requested voucher units, grouped by ingredient. This points to bottlenecks.
- Hoarding index: the largest pile of one foreign ingredient held by one player. This flags concentrated blockage.
- Liquidity depth: average number of distinct assets in the Common Basket after basket-moving transactions. Higher depth usually means easier matching.
- Settlement time: turns from the final prepared dish to all debts cleared and eating beginning.
- Inequality of consumption: variance in bites eaten per player. Zero means equal consumption.
- Trade balance per player: value received minus value given in direct and basket trades.

The live Game Stats panel shows the metrics that are reliable from the current compact snapshot and transaction summary. Scarcity pressure, hoarding index, and trade balance are useful analysis-package candidates, but they need stronger event/state instrumentation before they should be shown during play; otherwise they often read as `none` or all-zero and create confusion.

## Reading Results

A strong game economy tends to show:

- moderate-to-high basket velocity;
- low scarcity pressure;
- low hoarding index;
- short settlement time;
- positive productivity relative to assets lost;
- low consumption variance.

A struggling game economy tends to show:

- high settlement burden;
- high scarcity pressure for one ingredient;
- high hoarding index;
- low liquidity depth;
- long settlement time after all dishes are complete.

Those patterns usually mean the game needs better bot settlement behavior, clearer player tools for returning cards, or more visible information about who needs what.

## Analysis Package Direction

Future analysis work should treat the transaction history as an economic event log. Useful next outputs include:

- per-turn balance sheets;
- ingredient demand curves by recipe stage;
- asset-location timelines;
- settlement debt graphs;
- liquidity-depth time series;
- player-level productivity and exchange-network centrality.

These should be generated from exported transaction CSVs and, where needed, richer state snapshots. The live client should keep showing compact, readable summaries while deeper analysis lives in scripts and reports.

The first headless Monte Carlo analysis package lives in `analysis/economic-time/`.
It varies coordination delay, hoarding, last-ingredient reluctance, Common Basket
thinness, then writes reusable run-level CSVs, per-event timeline CSVs for
later animation, and final SVG/PNG figures for the production-to-clearance
cycle.
